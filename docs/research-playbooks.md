# Research playbooks

These playbooks organize experiments. They are not solution claims.

## Riemann Hypothesis

1. Retrieve the number-theory, spectral, automorphic, trace-formula, random-matrix and noncommutative-geometry neighborhood.
2. Separate proved explicit-formula facts from Hilbert–Pólya-style heuristics.
3. For every candidate operator, test domain, symmetry and self-adjointness before interpreting its spectrum.
4. Finitize one trace identity and state exactly which feature of zeta zeros it models.
5. Formal target: a finite matrix or finite Euler-product identity with no analytic continuation assumptions hidden in notation.

## Navier–Stokes regularity

1. Retrieve PDE, harmonic analysis, probability, optimal transport and scaling/renormalization nodes.
2. Identify the critical norm and the precise estimate that fails to close.
3. Test a finite-scale recursion for monotonicity or concentration.
4. Reject analogies that average away worst-case singular behavior.
5. Formal target: a discrete energy inequality with explicit constants and hypotheses.

## Yang–Mills existence and mass gap

1. Separate classical gauge geometry from constructive quantum field theory.
2. Specify which finite lattice or representation-theoretic object is intended to preserve a gap mechanism.
3. Check gauge invariance and the continuum-limit dependency independently.
4. Treat “spectral gap” and “mass gap” as a dictionary to justify, not synonyms.
5. Formal target: a finite gauge action and a proved positive gap for a bounded model.

## Hodge Conjecture

1. Retrieve algebraic geometry, topology, motives/periods and categorical transfer nodes.
2. Write the cycle/cohomology dictionary and coefficient field explicitly.
3. Test low-dimensional or finite analogues before invoking motivic language.
4. Record where the candidate transfer loses algebraicity.
5. Formal target: an explicit finite cycle-class map and one preserved relation.

## Birch and Swinnerton–Dyer

1. Retrieve arithmetic geometry, number theory and automorphic forms.
2. Separate algebraic rank, analytic rank and modularity inputs.
3. Test finite proxies only for the feature they actually preserve.
4. Avoid interpreting numerical vanishing as a proof of order of vanishing.
5. Formal target: a finite arithmetic invariant or explicit low-rank calculation already supported by definitions.

## P versus NP

1. Retrieve complexity theory, proof complexity/geometry and category theory.
2. Name the circuit, proof-system or reduction model before discussing lower bounds.
3. Test whether the proposed invariant survives efficient reductions and avoids known barrier patterns.
4. Demote categorical language when it forgets resource bounds.
5. Formal target: a finite language, reduction and invariant-preservation lemma.

## Poincaré calibration

Use the solved Poincaré Conjecture to test whether the graph can represent a genuine proof strategy without reconstructing the proof. The expected path should expose topology, differential geometry, Ricci flow and analytic control, while keeping the solved status explicit.
